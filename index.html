1.1 Units

Use the following units: kg, m, m/s, m/s², N, s, N/kg

In physics, correct units ensure clarity and accuracy:

  • Kilogram (kg): Mass
  • Metre (m): Distance/length
  • Metre/second (m/s): Speed/velocity
  • Metre/second² (m/s²): Acceleration
  • Newton (N): Force
  • Second (s): Time
  • Newton/kilogram (N/kg): Gravitational field strength

1.2P Additional Units

Understanding Newton Metre (Nm) and Kilogram Metre/Second (kg·m/s)

This slide explores two critical units in physics:

  • Newton Metre (Nm): A measure of a moment, or the turning effect of a force. It's calculated as:
    Moment (Nm) = Force (N) × Distance (m) from the pivot.
    Example: A force of 10 N applied 0.5 m from a pivot produces a moment of 5 Nm.
  • Kilogram Metre/Second (kg·m/s): A measure of momentum, which quantifies mass in motion. It's calculated as:
    Momentum (kg·m/s) = Mass (kg) × Velocity (m/s).
    Example: A 2 kg object moving at 3 m/s has a momentum of 6 kg·m/s.

Both these units help us understand key aspects of motion and force interactions in the physical world.

1.3 Distance–Time Graphs

Understanding and Interpreting Distance–Time Graphs

Distance–time graphs are a fundamental tool for analyzing motion. Here's how to interpret them:

  • Horizontal Line: The object is stationary (no change in distance).
  • Straight Sloped Line: Constant speed; the steeper the line, the faster the speed.
  • Curved Line: Acceleration or deceleration (changing speed).

Example: A car travels 20 meters in 4 seconds, then stops for 2 seconds, and finally moves another 10 meters in 2 seconds. This would produce a graph with:

  • A sloped line for the first 4 seconds.
  • A flat line for the next 2 seconds (stationary).
  • A less steep sloped line for the final 2 seconds.

1.4 Speed Calculation

Understanding and Calculating Average Speed

The formula for average speed is:
Average Speed = Total Distance ÷ Total Time

Example: A car covers 60 m in 10 seconds. Using the formula:
Average Speed = 60 ÷ 10 = 6 m/s.

Remember:

  • Units for distance are typically meters (m).
  • Units for time are seconds (s).
  • Speed is measured in meters per second (m/s).

Average speed gives an overall measure, but it doesn’t account for variations in speed during the trip.

1.6 Acceleration

Understanding and Calculating Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate at which velocity changes over time. It can be calculated using the formula:

Acceleration = (Final Velocity - Initial Velocity) ÷ Time
Or: a = (v - u) ÷ t

Acceleration can be:

  • Positive: When an object speeds up.
  • Negative (Deceleration): When an object slows down.
  • Zero: When an object moves at a constant velocity.

Example: A car accelerates from 5 m/s to 15 m/s in 2 seconds. Using the formula:
Acceleration = (15 - 5) ÷ 2 = 5 m/s².

1.8 Acceleration from Velocity–Time Graphs

Understanding the Gradient of a Velocity–Time Graph

The gradient (slope) of a velocity–time graph provides the acceleration of an object. Here's how to calculate it:

Acceleration = Change in Velocity ÷ Time Taken

  • Positive Gradient: Indicates acceleration (speeding up).
  • Negative Gradient: Indicates deceleration (slowing down).
  • Zero Gradient: Indicates constant velocity.

Example: A car’s velocity increases from 10 m/s to 30 m/s over 5 seconds. The gradient is:
Acceleration = (30 - 10) ÷ 5 = 4 m/s².

1.9 Distance from Velocity–Time Graphs

Calculating Distance Using the Area Under a Velocity–Time Graph

The distance traveled by an object is represented by the area under its velocity–time graph. This includes:

  • Rectangles: Calculate area using length × width.
  • Triangles: Calculate area using (1/2) × base × height.
  • Complex Shapes: Break them into simple shapes (rectangles and triangles) and sum the areas.

Example: A car moves at a constant velocity of 20 m/s for 5 seconds, then accelerates uniformly to 40 m/s over the next 5 seconds. The distance is calculated as:

  1. Distance for the first 5 seconds (rectangle):
    Area = 20 × 5 = 100 m
  2. Distance for the next 5 seconds (triangle):
    Area = (1/2) × 5 × (40 - 20) = 50 m
  3. Total Distance = 100 m + 50 m = 150 m

1.10 Equation of Motion

Understanding and Using the Equation of Motion

The equation of motion relates velocity, acceleration, and distance:
v² = u² + 2as

Where:

  • v: Final velocity (m/s)
  • u: Initial velocity (m/s)
  • a: Acceleration (m/s²)
  • s: Distance traveled (m)

Example: A car starts at rest (u = 0), accelerates at 2 m/s², and travels 8 m. Calculate its final velocity:
Using the formula:
v² = 0² + 2 × 2 × 8 = 32
v = √32 = 5.66 m/s.

1.11 Effects of Forces

Understanding the Effects of Forces on Objects

Forces can change the motion or shape of an object. The main effects of forces include:

  • Changing Speed: Forces can accelerate (speed up) or decelerate (slow down) objects.
  • Changing Direction: Forces can alter the direction of an object’s motion.
  • Changing Shape: Forces can compress, stretch, or deform objects.

Example: A football player kicks a stationary ball, applying force to change its speed and direction. Similarly, squeezing a rubber ball changes its shape.

1.12 Types of Forces

Exploring Different Types of Forces

Forces are interactions that can change the motion or shape of objects. Here are the main types:

  • Gravitational Force: Attraction between objects due to their masses (e.g., Earth's gravity pulling objects down).
  • Electrostatic Force: Force between charged particles (e.g., attraction or repulsion between electrons and protons).
  • Frictional Force: Opposes the motion of objects sliding or rolling over a surface.
  • Magnetic Force: Attraction or repulsion between magnetic poles (e.g., a magnet attracting iron nails).
  • Tension Force: Force exerted by a rope, string, or cable when pulled tight.
  • Air Resistance: A type of friction that opposes objects moving through air.
  • Normal Force: Perpendicular force exerted by a surface to support an object resting on it.

Example: When a book rests on a table, gravitational force pulls it downward, and the table exerts an equal normal force upward.

Quantities in physics can be classified as either scalars or vectors:

  • Scalars: Quantities with magnitude only (e.g., speed, distance, mass).
  • Vectors: Quantities with both magnitude and direction (e.g., velocity, force, displacement).

1.14 Force is a Vector

Understanding Why Force is a Vector

Force is a vector quantity because it has both:

  • Magnitude: The size or strength of the force (e.g., 10 N).
  • Direction: The direction in which the force is applied (e.g., pushing right).

Example: A 5 N force applied to the right and a 3 N force applied to the left result in a net force of 2 N to the right. The magnitude is 2 N, and the direction is to the right.

Forces acting on objects can be represented using arrows. The length of the arrow shows the magnitude, and the direction of the arrow shows the direction of the force.

1.15 Resultant Forces

Calculating and Understanding Resultant Forces

The resultant force is the single force that has the same effect as all the forces acting on an object. It determines the object’s motion (e.g., acceleration, deceleration, or constant speed). Here’s how to calculate it:

  • Forces in the Same Direction: Add their magnitudes.
  • Forces in Opposite Directions: Subtract the smaller force from the larger force. The resultant force points in the direction of the larger force.
  • Perpendicular Forces: Use the Pythagorean theorem to calculate the resultant force:
  • Resultant Force (R) = √(F₁² + F₂²)

Example 1: Two forces of 10 N and 5 N act to the right. Resultant force = 10 + 5 = 15 N to the right.

Example 2: A 10 N force acts to the right, and a 7 N force acts to the left. Resultant force = 10 - 7 = 3 N to the right.

Example 3: A 3 N force acts upward, and a 4 N force acts to the right. Resultant force = √(3² + 4²) = 5 N at an angle.

1.16 Friction

Understanding Friction and Its Effects

Friction is a force that opposes the motion of an object. It acts in the opposite direction to the motion or applied force and plays a critical role in everyday life.

Types of Friction:

  • Static Friction: Prevents an object from moving when a force is applied. It acts until the applied force exceeds its maximum limit.
  • Kinetic (Sliding) Friction: Opposes the motion of an object already in motion.
  • Rolling Friction: Occurs when an object rolls over a surface (e.g., a ball or a wheel).
  • Air Resistance: A type of friction that acts against objects moving through air.

Example: When you push a box, friction between the box and the floor resists the motion. If you stop pushing, friction brings the box to a halt.

1.17 F = m × a

Understanding Force, Mass, and Acceleration

The relationship between force, mass, and acceleration is given by the equation:

F = m × a

  • F: Force (measured in newtons, N).
  • m: Mass (measured in kilograms, kg).
  • a: Acceleration (measured in meters per second squared, m/s²).

This equation tells us that the force acting on an object is directly proportional to its mass and acceleration.

Example: A 2 kg object accelerates at 3 m/s². The force required is:
F = 2 × 3 = 6 N.

1.18 Weight Equation: \( W = m \cdot g \)

Understanding Weight, Mass, and Gravitational Field Strength

The relationship between weight (\( W \)), mass (\( m \)), and gravitational field strength (\( g \)) is expressed as:

\( W = m \cdot g \)

  • \( W \): Weight (measured in newtons, N).
  • \( m \): Mass (measured in kilograms, kg).
  • \( g \): Gravitational field strength (measured in newtons per kilogram, \( \text{N/kg} \)).

On Earth, \( g \approx 9.8 \, \text{N/kg} \), and on the Moon, \( g \approx 1.6 \, \text{N/kg} \). Weight varies with location because \( g \) changes, but mass remains constant.

Example: A 5 kg object on Earth has a weight of:

\( W = 5 \cdot 9.8 = 49 \, \text{N} \)

1.19 Stopping Distance

Understanding Stopping Distance

Stopping distance is the total distance a vehicle travels before coming to a complete stop. It is the sum of:

\[ \text{Stopping Distance} = \text{Thinking Distance} + \text{Braking Distance} \]

  • Thinking Distance: The distance the vehicle travels during the driver's reaction time (\( t \)).
  • Braking Distance: The distance the vehicle travels while the brakes are applied.

Example: A car moving at \( v = 20 \, \text{m/s} \) with a reaction time of \( t = 1 \, \text{s} \), and taking 30 meters to stop after braking.

\[ \text{Thinking Distance} = v \cdot t = 20 \cdot 1 = 20 \, \text{m} \] \[ \text{Braking Distance} = 30 \, \text{m} \] \[ \text{Stopping Distance} = 20 + 30 = 50 \, \text{m} \]

Factors affecting stopping distance:

  • Speed: Higher speeds increase both thinking and braking distance.
  • Reaction Time: Delayed reactions (e.g., tiredness or distractions) increase thinking distance.
  • Road Conditions: Wet, icy, or uneven roads increase braking distance.
  • Vehicle Condition: Poor brakes or worn tires increase braking distance.

1.20 Factors Affecting Stopping Distance

Key Factors Influencing Stopping Distance

Stopping distance is influenced by several factors that affect both thinking distance and braking distance. These include:

  • Speed: Higher speeds increase both thinking and braking distance. Braking distance increases quadratically with speed:

\[ \text{Braking Distance} \propto v^2 \]

  • Reaction Time: Affected by distractions, fatigue, alcohol, or drugs, longer reaction times increase thinking distance:

\[ \text{Thinking Distance} = v \cdot t \]

  • Road Conditions: Wet, icy, or uneven roads reduce traction, increasing braking distance.
  • Vehicle Condition: Worn tires or poor brakes reduce braking efficiency.
  • Mass: Heavier vehicles require more force to stop, increasing braking distance.

1.21 Terminal Velocity

Understanding Terminal Velocity

Terminal velocity occurs when the forces acting on a falling object are balanced, and the object moves at a constant speed. This happens when:

  • Weight (\( W \)): The gravitational force pulling the object downward.
  • Air Resistance (\( F_r \)): The upward force opposing the motion of the object.

At terminal velocity:

\[ W = F_r \]

As an object falls, air resistance increases with speed. Eventually, the air resistance equals the weight, resulting in no net force:

\[ F_{\text{net}} = W - F_r = 0 \]

The object continues to fall at a constant speed called the terminal velocity.

1.22 Practical: Measuring Extension

Understanding Force and Extension in Materials

When a force is applied to a material, it can stretch, compress, or deform. The relationship between the applied force (\( F \)) and the extension (\( x \)) is often linear within the elastic limit, as described by Hooke’s Law:

\[ F = k \cdot x \]

  • \( F \): Force (in newtons, N)
  • \( k \): Spring constant (in N/m), a measure of the material’s stiffness
  • \( x \): Extension (in meters, m)

To investigate this relationship, we measure the extension of a material (e.g., a spring) as weights are added incrementally. A graph of force (\( F \)) against extension (\( x \)) typically shows:

  • Linear Region: Proportionality between force and extension (\( F \propto x \)).
  • Elastic Limit: Beyond this point, the material no longer returns to its original shape after the force is removed.

1.23 Hooke's Law

Understanding Hooke's Law

Hooke’s Law describes the relationship between the force applied to an elastic material and its extension, as long as the material is within its elastic limit. The law is expressed as:

\[ F = k \cdot x \]

  • \( F \): Force (in newtons, N)
  • \( k \): Spring constant (in N/m), representing the stiffness of the material
  • \( x \): Extension (in meters, m)

Elastic Limit: If the force exceeds the elastic limit, the material will deform permanently and will not obey Hooke’s Law. Below the elastic limit, the force and extension are proportional:

\[ F \propto x \]

1.24 Elastic Behaviour

Understanding Elastic Behaviour

Elastic behaviour describes how materials return to their original shape after the force causing deformation is removed. This property is governed by the material’s elastic limit:

  • Below the Elastic Limit: The material behaves elastically, returning to its original shape when the force is removed.
  • Above the Elastic Limit: The material deforms permanently and no longer exhibits elastic behaviour.

The relationship between force and extension in the elastic region is often linear, as described by Hooke's Law:

\[ F = k \cdot x \]

Example: A rubber band stretches when pulled and returns to its original length when released, as long as the force does not exceed its elastic limit.

1.25 Momentum

Understanding Momentum

Momentum is a measure of the motion of an object and is calculated as the product of its mass and velocity:

\[ p = m \cdot v \]

  • \( p \): Momentum (in kilogram meters per second, \( \text{kg·m/s} \))
  • \( m \): Mass (in kilograms, \( \text{kg} \))
  • \( v \): Velocity (in meters per second, \( \text{m/s} \))

Example: A car with a mass of \( 1,000 \, \text{kg} \) traveling at a velocity of \( 20 \, \text{m/s} \) has a momentum of:

\[ p = 1,000 \cdot 20 = 20,000 \, \text{kg·m/s} \]

Momentum is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

1.26 Momentum and Safety

Reducing Impact Force with Longer Impact Time

The principle of reducing impact force by increasing impact time is critical in safety design. The force experienced during a collision is given by:

\[ F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} \]

  • \( F \): Force (in newtons, \( \text{N} \))
  • \( \Delta p \): Change in momentum (in \( \text{kg·m/s} \))
  • \( \Delta t \): Time over which the momentum changes (in seconds, \( \text{s} \))

By increasing the impact time (\( \Delta t \)), the force (\( F \)) experienced during a collision decreases. This principle is used in safety features like:

  • Airbags: Increase the time taken for a passenger's momentum to reach zero, reducing the force.
  • Seatbelts: Spread the force over a larger area and increase the stopping time.
  • Crumple Zones: Absorb energy and increase the time over which the car stops.

Example: A car with a momentum change (\( \Delta p \)) of \( 2,000 \, \text{kg·m/s} \) experiences this change in \( 0.5 \, \text{s} \). The force is:

\[ F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{2,000}{0.5} = 4,000 \, \text{N} \]

1.27 Conservation of Momentum

Understanding Momentum Conservation

The principle of conservation of momentum states that in a closed system, with no external forces acting, the total momentum before an interaction is equal to the total momentum after the interaction:

\[ \text{Total Momentum Before} = \text{Total Momentum After} \]

For a collision between two objects:

\[ m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 \]

  • \( m_1, m_2 \): Masses of objects 1 and 2 (in kilograms, \( \text{kg} \))
  • \( u_1, u_2 \): Initial velocities of objects 1 and 2 (in meters per second, \( \text{m/s} \))
  • \( v_1, v_2 \): Final velocities of objects 1 and 2 (in meters per second, \( \text{m/s} \))

Example: Two objects collide. Object 1 (\( m_1 = 2 \, \text{kg} \), \( u_1 = 3 \, \text{m/s} \)) collides with object 2 (\( m_2 = 4 \, \text{kg} \), \( u_2 = 0 \, \text{m/s} \)), and they stick together. The final velocity (\( v \)) is:

\[ m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v \] \[ 2 \cdot 3 + 4 \cdot 0 = (2 + 4) v \] \[ 6 = 6v \implies v = 1 \, \text{m/s} \]

1.28 Force and Momentum Change

Understanding Force and Momentum

The force acting on an object is related to the rate of change of its momentum. This relationship is expressed as:

\[ F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} \]

  • \( F \): Force (in newtons, \( \text{N} \))
  • \( \Delta p \): Change in momentum (in \( \text{kg·m/s} \))
  • \( \Delta t \): Time over which the momentum changes (in seconds, \( \text{s} \))

The change in momentum (\( \Delta p \)) can also be expressed as:

\[ \Delta p = m \cdot (v - u) \]

  • \( m \): Mass of the object (in \( \text{kg} \))
  • \( v \): Final velocity (in \( \text{m/s} \))
  • \( u \): Initial velocity (in \( \text{m/s} \))

Example: A car of mass \( 1,000 \, \text{kg} \) accelerates from \( 10 \, \text{m/s} \) to \( 30 \, \text{m/s} \) in \( 5 \, \text{s} \). The force is:

\[ \Delta p = m \cdot (v - u) = 1,000 \cdot (30 - 10) = 20,000 \, \text{kg·m/s} \] \[ F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{20,000}{5} = 4,000 \, \text{N} \]

1.29 Newton's Third Law

Understanding Newton's Third Law of Motion

Newton's Third Law of Motion states:

\[ \text{For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.} \]

This means that when one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object exerts an equal force in the opposite direction on the first object. These forces:

  • Are equal in magnitude
  • Act in opposite directions
  • Act on different objects

Example: When a swimmer pushes against the wall of a pool, the wall pushes back with an equal and opposite force, propelling the swimmer forward.

Mathematically, this is expressed as:

\[ F_{\text{action}} = -F_{\text{reaction}} \]

1.30 Moments

Understanding Moments

A moment is the turning effect of a force around a pivot point. It depends on two factors:

  • Force (\( F \)): The strength of the applied force (in newtons, \( \text{N} \)).
  • Distance (\( d \)): The perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force (in meters, \( \text{m} \)).

The formula for calculating moments is:

\[ \text{Moment} = F \cdot d \]

Example: A \( 10 \, \text{N} \) force is applied \( 0.5 \, \text{m} \) from the pivot. The moment is:

\[ \text{Moment} = 10 \cdot 0.5 = 5 \, \text{Nm} \]

1.31 Centre of Gravity

Understanding the Centre of Gravity

The centre of gravity of an object is the point at which its entire weight appears to act. For uniform objects, the centre of gravity is at the geometric center.

  • For Regular Shapes: The centre of gravity is at the center of symmetry.
  • For Irregular Shapes: The centre of gravity can be found experimentally by suspending the object and finding the balance point.

Example: The centre of gravity of a uniform rectangular plate lies at the intersection of its diagonals.

To maintain stability, the line of action of the weight (passing through the centre of gravity) should lie within the base of the object.

1.32 Principle of Moments

Understanding the Principle of Moments

The principle of moments states:

\[ \text{For equilibrium: Total clockwise moments = Total anticlockwise moments.} \]

A moment is calculated using:

\[ \text{Moment} = F \cdot d \]

  • \( F \): Force applied (in newtons, \( \text{N} \))
  • \( d \): Perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force (in meters, \( \text{m} \))

Example: A seesaw is balanced with a \( 20 \, \text{N} \) force applied \( 2 \, \text{m} \) from the pivot on one side. On the other side, the force is \( 10 \, \text{N} \). The distance from the pivot on the second side can be found as:

\[ F_1 \cdot d_1 = F_2 \cdot d_2 \] \[ 20 \cdot 2 = 10 \cdot d_2 \] \[ d_2 = \frac{40}{10} = 4 \, \text{m} \]

1.33 Upward Forces on a Beam

Understanding Upward Forces on a Beam

When a beam is supported at both ends and a load is applied, the upward forces provided by the supports depend on the position of the load. The total upward forces must balance the downward force (the load) to maintain equilibrium:

\[ R_1 + R_2 = W \]

  • \( R_1, R_2 \): Upward reaction forces at the supports (in \( \text{N} \))
  • \( W \): Weight of the load (in \( \text{N} \))

The moments around any point must also balance. For example, about the left support:

\[ R_2 \cdot d_2 = W \cdot d_1 \]

  • \( d_1, d_2 \): Distances from the load to the supports (in \( \text{m} \))

Example: A \( 100 \, \text{N} \) load is placed \( 2 \, \text{m} \) from the left support of a \( 5 \, \text{m} \) beam. The reactions are:

\[ R_1 + R_2 = 100 \] \[ R_2 \cdot 5 = 100 \cdot 2 \implies R_2 = \frac{200}{5} = 40 \, \text{N} \] \[ R_1 = 100 - 40 = 60 \, \text{N} \]
